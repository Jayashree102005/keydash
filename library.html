How Each Key Type Works

Understanding the technology behind your car key helps us serve you better.

Transponder Technology

RFID-based immobilizer system

When the key is inserted, the vehicle sends a radio signal to the transponder chip. The chip responds with a unique code. If the codes match, the engine starts. This prevents hot-wiring.

Compatible Brands

Toyota Honda Ford Chevrolet Nissan Hyundai

Smart / Proximity Technology

RF & NFC-based passive entry

The fob constantly broadcasts a low-frequency signal. When the car detects the fob within range (typically 1-2 meters), it authenticates the signal and unlocks the doors. Push-start works via encrypted RFID handshake.

Compatible Brands

BMW Mercedes Audi Lexus Porsche Tesla

Remote Keyless Entry

UHF radio frequency remotes

A remote fob transmits a coded UHF signal to the vehicle's receiver. A rolling code system ensures each press generates a unique signal, preventing code grabbing and replay attacks.

Typical Range

Up to 30-50 feet line of sight, depending on the vehicle and environment.

Traditional / Mechanical Keys

Physical key cutting only

These keys rely purely on the physical shape of the blade matching the lock cylinder wafers. No electronic authentication. Used primarily in pre-2000 vehicles and as emergency backup blades.

Cutting Methods

Edge-cut (standard) or laser-track (sidewinder) milling.

Identification

How To Identify Your Key Type

Not sure what kind of key you have? Use these simple clues.

1

Look At Blade

Is it a plain metal blade with cuts on one edge? That's a traditional key. Precision grooves on both sides? That's a laser-cut key.

2

Check The Head

A thick plastic head usually means there's a transponder chip inside. If the key has buttons, it's a remote fob.

3

Start Your Car

Do you push a button to start? That's a smart/proximity system. Do you turn a key? It's traditional or transponder.
